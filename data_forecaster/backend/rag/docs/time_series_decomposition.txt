Time Series Decomposition Methods and Interpretation

Time series decomposition separates a series into interpretable structural components:
trend, seasonality, and residuals (irregular fluctuations). Decomposition is both
an analytical tool (to understand the series) and a preprocessing step (to inform
model selection).

The Three Core Components

1. Trend (T): The long-run direction of the series. Can be linear (constant rate of
   change), nonlinear, or absent. A rising trend in retail sales over years is an
   example.

2. Seasonality (S): Repeating patterns at a fixed period — the same period every
   year, week, or day. Distinct from cycles, which have variable period lengths.
   Example: airline passengers peak every summer.

3. Residual / Irregular (R): What remains after removing trend and seasonality.
   Should behave like white noise if the decomposition is adequate. Persistent
   structure in the residual indicates the model is missing something.

Additive vs Multiplicative Decomposition

Additive model: y_t = T_t + S_t + R_t
- Appropriate when the seasonal variation is roughly constant in absolute terms
  regardless of the level of the series.
- Example: a store that always sells approximately 200 extra units in December.

Multiplicative model: y_t = T_t * S_t * R_t
- Appropriate when seasonal variation scales proportionally with the trend level.
- Example: a store whose December sales are 30% above its monthly average, which
  grows as the store's overall sales grow.
- Often implemented by log-transforming the series: log(y_t) converts multiplicative
  decomposition to additive.

Rule of thumb: if the amplitude of seasonal swings increases as the series grows,
use multiplicative (or log + additive). If it stays constant, use additive.

Classical Decomposition (Moving Average Based)
The oldest approach. Computes trend via a centred moving average, then derives
seasonal indices by averaging the detrended values over all seasons.

Limitations:
- Trend is undefined at the start and end of the series (the moving average window
  needs data on both sides).
- Assumes seasonal patterns are constant over time — cannot handle evolving seasonality.
- Sensitive to outliers.

STL Decomposition (Seasonal-Trend decomposition using LOESS)
STL is the recommended modern approach. It uses locally-weighted regression (LOESS)
to estimate trend and season components iteratively, handling:
- Non-constant (evolving) seasonality.
- Robust fitting that downweights outliers.
- Any seasonal period (not limited to specific frequencies).

Key STL parameters:
- seasonal_window (s-window): controls how rapidly the seasonal component is allowed
  to change. A larger value = smoother, more stable seasonality. Setting to 'periodic'
  forces constant seasonality across the series.
- trend_window: controls the smoothness of the trend. Automatically set based on
  the seasonal period if not specified. A longer window = smoother trend.
- robustness: when enabled, outliers are identified and downweighted, making the
  decomposition more resistant to anomalies.

Interpreting STL Output
- Trend panel: should show the underlying direction free of seasonal noise. If it
  is nearly flat, the series has no meaningful trend.
- Seasonal panel: the repeating pattern extracted at each period. If this is
  approximately zero, there is no seasonality. Compare its scale to the trend and
  residual panels.
- Residual panel: ideally random with no obvious structure. Check by plotting the
  ACF of the residuals — all spikes should be within the significance bands.
- Strength of seasonality: Fs = max(0, 1 - Var(R) / Var(S + R)). Values > 0.6
  indicate strong seasonality.
- Strength of trend: Ft = max(0, 1 - Var(R) / Var(T + R)). Values > 0.6 indicate
  a strong trend.

X-11 and X-13ARIMA-SEATS
These are the official decomposition methods used by statistical agencies (e.g. the
US Census Bureau). They apply a series of moving averages and ARIMA models to
handle trading day effects, holidays, and outliers. They are more complex than STL
but produce higher-quality results for official economic statistics.

Practical Uses of Decomposition
1. Model selection: strong trend → ARIMA/Holt-Winters trend component needed.
   Strong seasonality → SARIMA or Holt-Winters with seasonal component.
   Both present → SARIMA or triple exponential smoothing (Holt-Winters).
2. Outlier detection: unusually large residuals indicate anomalies or data errors.
3. Seasonally adjusted series: subtracting (or dividing by) the seasonal component
   removes seasonal effects, making year-on-year changes clearer.
4. Forecasting: some methods forecast trend and seasonality separately, then
   recombine. Holt-Winters is a direct implementation of this idea.

Seasonality Strength as a Model Selection Signal
- Fs > 0.6: seasonal models (SARIMA, Holt-Winters) are likely superior to ARIMA.
- Fs < 0.3: seasonality is weak; ARIMA may be sufficient.
- 0.3 < Fs < 0.6: borderline — compare both approaches using AIC/cross-validation.
