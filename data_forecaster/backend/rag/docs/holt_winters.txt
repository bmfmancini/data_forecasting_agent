Holt-Winters Exponential Smoothing Methodology

Holt-Winters (also called Triple Exponential Smoothing) is a classical forecasting method that
models three components of a time series: level, trend, and seasonality. It is intuitive,
computationally efficient, and performs well on a broad range of business data.

Components:
1. Level (l_t): The smoothed estimate of the series value at time t.
2. Trend (b_t): The smoothed estimate of the direction and rate of change.
3. Seasonal (s_t): The smoothed periodic factor for each season.

Seasonal Modes:
- Additive: seasonal fluctuations are roughly constant in magnitude regardless of the level.
  Appropriate when the seasonal swing does not grow with the series (e.g., temperature anomalies).
- Multiplicative: seasonal fluctuations scale proportionally with the level.
  Appropriate when the seasonal amplitude grows as the series grows (e.g., airline passengers).

Smoothing Parameters:
- alpha (α): level smoothing factor [0, 1]. High α gives more weight to recent observations.
- beta (β): trend smoothing factor [0, 1]. High β makes the trend more responsive to changes.
- gamma (γ): seasonal smoothing factor [0, 1]. Controls how fast the seasonal estimate adapts.

These parameters are optimised during fitting by minimising the sum of squared errors (SSE).
statsmodels ExponentialSmoothing performs this optimisation automatically.

When to use Holt-Winters:
- Series with a clear trend and stable seasonality.
- When visual inspection shows repeating seasonal patterns of consistent shape.
- When a simple, interpretable model is preferred over ARIMA/SARIMA.
- Short to medium-term forecasts (1–2 seasonal cycles ahead).

Advantages:
- Requires no stationarity pre-processing.
- Fast to fit even on long series.
- Handles both additive and multiplicative seasonality natively.
- Interpretable parameters with direct business meaning.

Limitations:
- Less flexible than ARIMA/SARIMA for complex autocorrelation structures.
- Assumes the trend is linear (Damped Holt-Winters can handle non-linear trends).
- Multiplicative mode requires strictly positive series values (cannot handle zeros/negatives).
- Prediction intervals are approximate (derived analytically from the model error variance).

Choosing Additive vs Multiplicative:
- If the seasonal amplitude is roughly constant over time → Additive.
- If the seasonal amplitude grows proportionally with the level → Multiplicative.
- When uncertain, fit both and compare AIC.

Evaluation Metrics: RMSE, MAE, MAPE (same framework as ARIMA — see arima.txt).

Relationship to Other Methods:
- Simple Exponential Smoothing is a special case with no trend and no seasonality.
- Holt's Linear Trend method is a special case with trend but no seasonality.
- Holt-Winters is the full three-component model used for trended, seasonal data.
