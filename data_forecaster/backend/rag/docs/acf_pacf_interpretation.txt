ACF and PACF Interpretation for Time Series Model Selection

The AutoCorrelation Function (ACF) and Partial AutoCorrelation Function (PACF) are
the primary diagnostic tools for identifying the order of ARIMA and SARIMA models.
Reading them correctly is essential for model selection.

What is Autocorrelation?
Autocorrelation measures the correlation between a time series and a lagged version
of itself. At lag k, the ACF value r(k) is the Pearson correlation between y_t and
y_{t-k} across all valid time points.

Key property: for a wide-sense stationary process, the ACF is symmetric (r(k) = r(-k))
and always achieves its maximum at lag 0, where r(0) = 1.

What is the Partial Autocorrelation Function?
The PACF at lag k measures the correlation between y_t and y_{t-k} after removing
the linear effect of all intermediate lags 1 through k-1. It isolates the direct
relationship at exactly lag k.

Significance Bounds
Both ACF and PACF plots display confidence bands (typically ±1.96/sqrt(n) for 95%
confidence). Spikes that exceed these bounds are statistically significant.

Important caveat: testing many lags simultaneously inflates the false positive rate.
Expect approximately 5% of lags to appear significant by chance even in white noise.

Pattern Recognition for AR, MA, and ARMA Models

AR(p) process — AutoRegressive of order p:
- ACF: decays exponentially or in a damped sinusoidal pattern (never cuts off sharply).
- PACF: cuts off sharply after lag p (significant spikes at lags 1..p only).
- Implication: the PACF directly indicates the AR order.

MA(q) process — Moving Average of order q:
- ACF: cuts off sharply after lag q (significant spikes at lags 1..q only).
- PACF: decays exponentially (never cuts off sharply).
- Implication: the ACF directly indicates the MA order.

ARMA(p,q) process:
- ACF: decays exponentially after lag q.
- PACF: decays exponentially after lag p.
- Neither cuts off sharply — use information criteria (AIC/BIC) instead of plots.

Common ACF/PACF Patterns and Their Interpretations

1. All spikes within bounds → white noise; no autocorrelation. Series may already
   be well-modelled or no model is needed.

2. ACF decays slowly (many significant lags, gradual decline) → non-stationary.
   The series needs differencing before fitting ARIMA. This is the most common
   pattern for economic and financial time series.

3. Large negative ACF spike at lag 1, rest within bounds → over-differenced.
   Reduce the differencing order.

4. PACF spike at lag 1 only, ACF decay → AR(1) process. One autoregressive term.

5. ACF spike at lag 1 only, PACF decay → MA(1) process. One moving average term.

6. ACF and PACF both have significant spikes at multiples of s (seasonal period) →
   seasonal component present. SARIMA or Holt-Winters is more appropriate than ARIMA.

7. ACF spike at lag s, PACF spikes decaying at lags s, 2s, 3s → seasonal MA(1).

8. PACF spike at lag s, ACF spikes decaying at lags s, 2s, 3s → seasonal AR(1).

Using ACF/PACF with ARIMA
After identifying d (differencing order) via ADF/KPSS:
1. Compute ACF and PACF on the differenced series.
2. Use the patterns above to identify candidate (p, q) pairs.
3. Fit competing models and compare AIC/BIC. Lower is better; prefer parsimony.
4. After fitting, check the residual ACF — it should show no significant spikes
   (residuals should be white noise). If significant spikes remain, the model is
   under-specified.

Ljung-Box Test
The Ljung-Box test is a formal statistical test for residual autocorrelation. It
tests the joint null hypothesis that a group of autocorrelations up to lag h are
all zero. A p-value > 0.05 means residuals are consistent with white noise —
a necessary condition for a well-fitted model.

Seasonality Detection via ACF
If the ACF shows significant spikes repeating at regular intervals (lags s, 2s, 3s,
etc.) the series has a seasonal component of period s. For monthly data this is
typically s=12; for quarterly s=4; for weekly s=52 or s=7.

The periodogram (spectral analysis) is an alternative tool for detecting seasonality:
dominant peaks in the power spectrum correspond to cyclic frequencies, and their
inverse gives the period in number of time steps.
