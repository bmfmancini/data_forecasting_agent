ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) Methodology

ARIMA is one of the most widely used statistical models for time series forecasting. It generalises
two simpler models — AR (AutoRegressive) and MA (Moving Average) — and adds differencing to handle
non-stationary series.

Model Notation: ARIMA(p, d, q)

- p (AutoRegressive order): the number of lagged values of the series included as predictors.
  Chosen by inspecting the Partial AutoCorrelation Function (PACF). Significant spikes at lags 1..p
  with a sharp cut-off suggest an AR(p) component.

- d (Integration order): the number of times the series must be differenced to achieve stationarity.
  A series is stationary when its mean, variance, and autocorrelation structure are constant over
  time. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test and KPSS test are the standard tools to determine d.
  Typically d=0 (already stationary), d=1 (first difference), or d=2 (second difference).

- q (Moving Average order): the number of lagged forecast errors included in the model.
  Chosen by inspecting the AutoCorrelation Function (ACF). Significant spikes at lags 1..q with a
  sharp cut-off suggest an MA(q) component.

Model Assumptions:
1. Residuals should be white noise (zero mean, constant variance, no autocorrelation).
2. The series must be made stationary before fitting (handled by differencing d times).
3. ARIMA does not model seasonality — use SARIMA for seasonal data.

Parameter Selection:
Modern practice uses pmdarima's auto_arima() function, which performs a stepwise search over a grid
of (p, d, q) values and selects the combination that minimises the AIC (Akaike Information Criterion)
or BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion). AIC penalises model complexity to prevent overfitting.

When to use ARIMA:
- Series with no clear seasonality.
- Short to medium forecast horizons (days to weeks).
- After differencing confirms non-stationarity can be removed.
- When ADF test rejects the unit-root hypothesis after d differencing steps.

Confidence Intervals:
ARIMA produces prediction intervals that widen as the forecast horizon increases, reflecting
accumulating uncertainty. 95% intervals are standard.

Evaluation Metrics:
- RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error): penalises large errors heavily.
- MAE (Mean Absolute Error): average absolute deviation; interpretable in original units.
- MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error): scale-independent; suitable for comparing across datasets.

Limitations:
- Assumes linear relationships between past and future values.
- Sensitive to outliers, which inflate error variance.
- Does not capture seasonal cycles — SARIMA or Holt-Winters should be used instead.
- Requires a sufficient history (at least 30–50 observations recommended).
