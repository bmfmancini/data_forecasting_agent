Forecast Error Metrics: Measurement, Interpretation, and Comparison

Evaluating forecast quality requires quantitative error metrics. Different metrics
capture different aspects of accuracy and each has specific strengths and weaknesses.
Choosing the right metric is as important as choosing the right model.

Notation
- y_t: actual observed value at time t
- f_t: forecasted value at time t
- e_t = y_t - f_t: forecast error (positive = under-forecast, negative = over-forecast)
- n: number of forecast periods evaluated

Core Point-Accuracy Metrics

Mean Error (ME)
ME = (1/n) * sum(e_t)
- Measures forecast bias. A positive ME means the model systematically under-forecasts;
  negative means over-forecasting.
- Positive and negative errors cancel out, so ME near zero does not guarantee accuracy.
- Always compute ME alongside magnitude metrics.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
MAE = (1/n) * sum(|e_t|)
- Average absolute magnitude of errors, in the same units as the series.
- Robust to outliers — large single errors are not disproportionately penalised.
- Easy to interpret: "on average, forecasts are off by X units."
- Preferred when large errors are not disproportionately costly.

Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)
RMSE = sqrt((1/n) * sum(e_t^2))
- Also in the same units as the series.
- Penalises large errors more heavily than MAE due to squaring.
- Preferred when large errors are particularly undesirable (e.g. inventory stockouts).
- More sensitive to outliers than MAE.
- Relationship: RMSE >= MAE always. When they are equal, all errors are the same size.
  A large RMSE/MAE ratio indicates the presence of occasional large errors.

Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE)
MAPE = (100/n) * sum(|e_t / y_t|)
- Scale-free: expressed as a percentage, so it can be compared across series with
  different units or magnitudes.
- Major weakness: undefined or extremely large when y_t = 0 or near zero.
- Asymmetric: a 50% over-forecast and a 50% under-forecast are not treated equally
  in terms of the underlying absolute errors.
- Biased towards forecasts that systematically under-predict (under-predictions can
  produce at most 100% MAPE; over-predictions are unbounded).
- Use with caution for series containing zeros or very small values.

Symmetric MAPE (sMAPE)
sMAPE = (100/n) * sum(2*|e_t| / (|y_t| + |f_t|))
- Addresses some asymmetry in MAPE by using the average of actual and forecast as
  the denominator.
- Still problematic when both actual and forecast are near zero.
- Used in the M-competition series of forecasting competitions.

Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE)
MASE = MAE / MAE_naive
where MAE_naive is the MAE of a naive one-step-ahead seasonal (or non-seasonal) forecast.
- Scale-free and well-defined even when values are zero.
- Values < 1 mean the model outperforms the naive benchmark.
- Values > 1 mean the model is worse than naive forecasting.
- Preferred metric for cross-series comparison and competition benchmarking.
- Less intuitive than MAE for non-technical audiences.

Directional Accuracy
DA = (1/n) * sum(1 if sign(y_t - y_{t-1}) == sign(f_t - f_{t-1}) else 0)
- Percentage of time steps where the forecast correctly predicted the direction of change.
- Important in financial applications where the sign of change matters more than magnitude.
- A value of 50% is equivalent to random guessing for a stationary series.

Choosing the Right Metric

Situation → Recommended Metric
- General-purpose reporting → MAE or RMSE
- When outliers/large errors must be heavily penalised → RMSE
- When all errors carry equal importance → MAE
- Comparing across different series/units → MAPE or MASE
- Series contains zeros → MASE (never MAPE)
- When benchmark comparison matters → MASE
- Stakeholder communication → MAPE (if no zeros), MAE

Evaluation Protocol: In-Sample vs Out-of-Sample

In-sample (training) error: error on data used to fit the model. Always lower than
out-of-sample due to overfitting. Never use in-sample error alone to assess forecast quality.

Out-of-sample (test) error: the gold standard. Evaluate on a held-out portion of
the series that the model never saw during fitting. Common splits: last 20% of
observations, or a rolling-origin evaluation.

Rolling-origin (time series cross-validation): fit the model on data up to time t,
forecast h steps ahead, record errors, advance t by one step, repeat. Produces
many evaluation windows and gives a more reliable estimate of forecast accuracy
than a single train/test split.

Information Criteria for Model Selection
AIC (Akaike Information Criterion) = 2k - 2*ln(L)
BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) = k*ln(n) - 2*ln(L)
where k = number of parameters, L = maximised likelihood, n = sample size.
- Lower AIC/BIC = better model (penalises complexity).
- BIC penalises complexity more strongly than AIC and tends to select more parsimonious models.
- Use for comparing models on the same dataset; not comparable across different datasets.
- AIC is preferred when forecasting is the goal; BIC is preferred when identifying
  the true data-generating process is the goal.

Residual Diagnostics
A well-specified model should have residuals that are:
1. Uncorrelated: ACF of residuals shows no significant spikes (verified with Ljung-Box test).
2. Zero mean: systematic bias (nonzero ME) suggests a missing component.
3. Homoskedastic: constant variance over time. Changing variance suggests GARCH effects.
4. Normally distributed: required for valid prediction intervals (verify with
   Shapiro-Wilk or Q-Q plot). Some models are robust to non-normality for point
   forecasts but interval coverage will be inaccurate.
Violation of any of these conditions indicates model inadequacy.
