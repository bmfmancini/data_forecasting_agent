Stationarity and Differencing in Time Series Analysis

Stationarity is a fundamental assumption for many forecasting models, including 
ARIMA and SARIMA. A stationary time series is one whose statistical properties 
(mean, variance, and autocorrelation) are constant over time.

Types of Stationarity:
1. Strict Stationarity: The entire distribution is invariant to time shifts.
2. Weak (Trend) Stationarity: Mean and variance are constant, and autocovariance 
   depends only on the lag, not the time.

Testing for Stationarity:
- Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test: Tests the null hypothesis that a unit root 
  is present (series is non-stationary). A p-value < 0.05 suggests stationarity.
- KPSS Test: Tests the null hypothesis that the series is stationary. A 
  p-value > 0.05 suggests stationarity.

Interpreting Conflicting Results:
- Stationary (ADF < 0.05, KPSS > 0.05): No action needed.
- Non-stationary (ADF > 0.05, KPSS < 0.05): Series has a unit root; differencing 
  is required.
- Trend Stationary (ADF > 0.05, KPSS > 0.05): The series appears non-stationary 
  due to a deterministic trend. Removing the trend makes it stationary.
- Difference Stationary (ADF < 0.05, KPSS < 0.05): The series is stationary 
  around a stochastic trend.

Differencing:
If a series is non-stationary, differencing is the primary tool to achieve 
stationarity:
- First-order differencing (d=1): y'_t = y_t - y_{t-1}. Effective for removing 
  linear trends.
- Second-order differencing (d=2): y''_t = y'_t - y'_{t-1}. Used when the trend 
  is changing over time.
- Seasonal differencing (D=1): y'_t = y_t - y_{t-m}, where m is the seasonal 
  period. Effective for removing seasonal patterns.

Over-differencing:
Applying differencing to an already stationary series can introduce artificial 
autocorrelation (moving average patterns) and increase forecast error. 
Models should use the minimum degree of differencing necessary to achieve 
a stable mean.
